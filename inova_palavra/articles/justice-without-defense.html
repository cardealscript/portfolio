Justice Without Defense

By Cardeal Felisberto | 2026

In Mozambique, justice often arrives without defense. In courtrooms from Maputo to provincial towns, the poor stand accused with no lawyer by their side. They face judges, prosecutors, and a system that speaks a language they cannot understand.

It is a cruel paradox: the Constitution promises equality before the law, yet in practice, justice is a privilege reserved for those who can afford it. The wealthy hire lawyers to argue, appeal, and delay. The poor, abandoned by the system, are pressured to confess crimes they did not commit.

Every day, men and women are condemned not because of what they did, but because of what they lacked: a lawyer, a voice, a chance. The justice system, instead of protecting the vulnerable, becomes a machine that crushes them.

"A justice system that condemns without defense is not justice. It is violence carried out in the name of law."

The courtroom becomes a theater where guilt is scripted in advance. This is not justice — it is survival. For the poor, the choice is between fighting a battle they cannot win or accepting guilt to shorten their suffering.

Justice without defense is not justice at all. It is punishment disguised as law. It is oppression disguised as order. And it is a reminder that in Mozambique, the poor are not only economically excluded — they are legally abandoned.
